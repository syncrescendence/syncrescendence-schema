## Part 1 — Calibration

**Whether Acts 1 and 2 deserve their own draft artifacts.** Yes, and the structural argument is straightforward. The reason Act 3 was extracted into DRAFT-STAGE-A2-COMMISSION.md was not that it was uniquely complex — it was that embedding future-act material inside the singular present-state artifact creates sovereignty ambiguity under bounded-context loading. The same argument applies to Acts 1 and 2. They are governance decisions with distinct preconditions, consequences, and reversibility profiles. Currently they exist only as prose sections within the handoff, which means a cold-start builder must parse the handoff to discover what governance acts are pending, reconstruct their sequence from inline text, and distinguish present state from pending governance objects embedded in the same document. Extracting each act into its own draft artifact means the handoff becomes a clean manifest: here is the present state, here are three pending governance acts, each at this named location, in this order. The handoff points to them; it does not contain them.

**Whether the handoff is correctly bounded without the extraction.** It functions, but it carries two categories of content that should be structurally distinct: present state (what is in force, what is pending, what the governance posture is) and pending governance acts (operator decisions with preconditions, consequences, and confirmation requirements). The current handoff's "Pending Governance Acts" section is a hybrid — it describes the acts but also partially constitutes them. Extraction sharpens the boundary: the handoff describes the situation and points to the acts; each act artifact specifies its own terms.

**Whether any reason exists to defer.** None. The acts are already fully specified in the handoff. Extraction is a packaging operation, not a content-creation operation. No new analysis is required.

**Drift risks without extraction.** The primary risk is *act-conflation under cognitive load*. An operator reading acceptance terms, method-advancement terms, and next-work-commission terms in a single document faces pressure to process them as one decision. Three separate artifacts, each requiring distinct confirmation, structurally enforce the three-act sequence. The secondary risk is *handoff bloat* — the more governance-act detail the handoff carries inline, the harder it becomes to scan for actual present state under bounded context.
