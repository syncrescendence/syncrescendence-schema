## Part 1 — Synthesis Readiness Calibration

**Whether eight surfaces are sufficient.** More than sufficient. The cross-entity synthesis method requires a minimum of three surface scaffolds; eight provides a comprehensive base that makes convergence/divergence analysis structurally meaningful. The eight surfaces span the full architectural spectrum of the SaaS class: messaging hub (Slack), recursive block-database (Notion), workflow FSM (Jira), enterprise wiki (Confluence), opinionated tracker (Linear), programmable document (Coda), converged work OS (ClickUp), and relational database-first platform (Airtable). This diversity means convergent primitives that survive across 5+ of these genuinely represent the SaaS class's structural consensus, not coincidental overlap between similar tools.

**Structural risks.** Two risks warrant attention. First, *false convergence inflation*: calling something "convergent" when it appears in a surface form that is structurally dissimilar across surfaces (e.g., "automation" exists in all eight but ranges from Jira's workflow FSM with transition logic to Linear's simple rule engine — are these the same primitive?). Mitigation: convergent primitives must share structural role, not just feature name. Where the structural character differs enough to warrant separate primitives, the feature belongs in the divergent zone. Second, *synthesis-zone thinness*: the synthesis zone must contain observations not derivable from any single surface scaffold. The temptation is to restate what each surface does well rather than identifying structural patterns that emerge only from cross-entity comparison. Mitigation: every synthesis observation must cite at least two surface scaffolds as evidence and state what structural insight the comparison reveals.

**What constitutes successful completion.** One enduring layer scaffold at Level 1 (curated) with all three mandatory zones substantively populated, passing the twelve-check validation schema with layer-scaffold species-specific instantiation on Checks 2, 4, and 10. The convergent zone must identify primitives shared across the majority (5+) of the eight surfaces. The divergent zone must cover all eight surfaces. The synthesis zone must contain observations not derivable from any single surface. The cross-entity synthesis method advances from Level 0 to "tested-once" (Level 1 validation requires at least one successful invocation with output passing validation).

**What would justify local repair without stopping.** Missing a convergent primitive that should have been identified (analogous to the Events API gap in Slack's scaffold) — add it. A convergent primitive that is claimed as shared but fails the structural-role test on inspection — reclassify it. A synthesis observation that on review is derivable from a single surface — remove or reclassify. None of these stop the invocation; they are bounded defect corrections within the production pass.

The synthesis zone is where the cross-entity comparison produces genuine new structural knowledge. Three observations deserve particular attention. First, `[saas-platform:flexibility-entropy-tension]` identifies a structural law of the modality class: no surface achieves both high flexibility and low entropy without active human governance. Notion's recursive freedom creates structure entropy; Airtable's schema-on-the-fly creates schema sprawl; Linear avoids entropy by restricting flexibility. This is not visible from any single scaffold. Second, `[saas-platform:lock-in-scales-with-structural-depth]` identifies that migration cost correlates with relational/logic complexity, not data volume — Coda's logic lock-in is the extreme case where the platform's entire value is non-portable computation. Third, `[saas-platform:three-paradigm-families]` clusters the eight surfaces into stream (Slack), document (Notion, Confluence, Coda), and tracker (Jira, Linear, ClickUp, Airtable) families, explaining why certain surfaces complement each other and why convergence platforms (ClickUp, Notion) attempt to span families.
