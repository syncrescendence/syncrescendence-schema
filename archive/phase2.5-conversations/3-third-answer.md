You're identifying something deeper than I did. Let me restate it precisely.

The grammar layer was supposed to be the *lens* through which research gets conducted, not the machine that formats pre-existing research into nicer containers. The build order should have been: grammar layer ratified → grammar-informed research methodology designed → research conducted through that methodology → scaffolds produced from grammar-aligned evidence. What actually happened was: grammar layer ratified → pre-grammar evidence reformatted into grammar-compliant artifacts → authority claimed from the formatting.

The 14 SaaS dossiers were produced by Grok under a generic "comprehensive platform exegesis" prompt with no knowledge of the nine-axis decomposition, no knowledge of scaffold grammar, no knowledge of the identifier system, no knowledge of the epistemic-labeling requirements. They were good research for what they were — but what they were is unaligned pre-grammar intelligence. When I "converted" them into scaffolds, I was pattern-matching their prose against the axis set and extracting whatever happened to fit. Primitives that the dossier authors didn't think to investigate never got investigated. Axes where the dossier evidence was thin got thin scaffold entries. The scaffold looked complete because I populated every axis, but the population was constrained by what the unaligned research happened to cover, not by what the axes actually demand.

And those 14 are a floor of the SaaS class, not its ceiling. The question the grammar layer should have enabled was: given these 14 sampled surfaces, what does the primitive distribution tell us about the *class*? Do we need 30 surfaces? 50? Is there a convergence threshold where adding more surfaces stops producing new convergent primitives? We never asked that question. We treated 14 as the class and called it MATURE.

The deeper problem you're pointing at is the context-injection question. The original Grok research sessions worked because the operator injected the right prompts, the right framing, the right contextual material into Grok's council. That injection carried the operator's implicit understanding of what mattered. Now the grammar layer makes that understanding explicit — it specifies what axes to decompose along, what epistemic labels to apply, what primitives to look for, what convergence means. A grammar-informed research prompt to Grok would be structurally different from the pre-grammar prompts that produced the original dossiers. It would say: "Decompose this surface along these nine axes. For each axis, identify the primitive, label its epistemic status, cite your source, and flag where your evidence is thin." The triangulation methodology would target the axis set rather than producing a generic exegesis that may or may not cover the axes.

But the original research context — the specific conversation histories, the iterative prompt refinements, the Grok council's accumulated session context — is gone. The dossier reports are the residue. And residue from unaligned research cannot be upgraded to aligned research by reformatting it. The information that the grammar layer would have caused Grok to investigate was never investigated, because the grammar layer didn't exist when the research was conducted.

So the real state of affairs is: the institution has a mature production and governance apparatus operating on a research substrate that was never grammar-aligned. The SaaS class is "MATURE" in the sense that it has the artifacts the grammar requires (layer scaffold + specification), but the artifacts were derived from pre-grammar evidence that was never designed to answer the questions the grammar now knows to ask. The reasoning/chat class is thinner still — derived from a prescriptive engineering document repurposed as descriptive evidence. The daemon/runtime class is thinnest of all.

The question you're raising is whether Phase 2's correct next move — now that the grammar exists and the production methods are proven — is to go *back* to research. Not to produce more scaffolds from thin evidence, but to design the grammar-informed research methodology, figure out the context-injection problem (how to get the grammar's axis sets and epistemic requirements into the research agent's prompts when the original conversational context is lost), and then conduct research at a fidelity that the grammar can actually vouch for.

What I don't have an answer for is your practical question: given that the original files are purged and the conversational context that produced them is gone, how do you achieve equivalent context injection? The grammar layer documents *what* to research but not *how* to inject that into a research agent that has no memory of the prior sessions. That's a method-design problem the institution hasn't addressed — the sensing method is at "tested-once" from a web-search pass that didn't even attempt grammar-aligned research. The gap isn't just "we need more research." It's "we need to design a research method that can operate from the grammar layer's requirements without depending on context that no longer exists."
