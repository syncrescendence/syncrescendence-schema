## Part 1: NAMING-AND-ADDRESSING.md Ratification Review Frame

Five checks, ordered by downstream propagation risk.

**Check 1 — Dual-form surface identifiers.** § 2.1 declares two forms: registry form (`slack_surface`) and slug form (`slack`). The question is whether this is justified complexity or premature engineering. Justification test: do the two forms serve structurally different purposes that a single form cannot? The registry form appears in the Teleology Registry as an institution-wide catalog entry with vendor disambiguation (`claude_anthropic_surface` vs. a hypothetical `claude_azure_surface`). The slug form appears inside composite identifiers where brevity matters — `[slack:channel-object-model]` vs. the unreadable `[slack_surface:channel-object-model]`. These are genuinely different contexts with different constraints: the registry needs verbose disambiguation, the composition context needs compact readability. A single form would force one context to absorb the other's constraints. The dual-form design also matches what the corpus already does — the teleology registry uses `snake_case_surface` while the naming law specifies `[surface-slug:primitive-name]` with implicit short slugs. The document codifies existing practice rather than inventing new complexity. **Risk level: clean.** The dual form is justified by the structural difference between catalog context and composition context. The critical constraint is that the mapping is declared once (in the registry entry) and stable — § 2.1 specifies this.

**Check 2 — Authority/lifecycle as metadata.** § 2.7 keeps authority levels and lifecycle states in artifact header blocks rather than encoding them in identifiers. The tradeoff: identifiers survive promotions and retirements without changing (good for referential stability), but you cannot determine authority from a filename alone (requires reading the header). Downstream impact test: does any downstream document need authority encoded in the identifier? AUTHORITY-LATTICE.md will specify decision rights per authority level — it needs to *query* authority, not *parse* it from filenames. READINESS-MATRIX.md will track maturity — same need for queryable metadata, not identifier parsing. The ontology projection layer will map artifacts to typed entities — it reads metadata, not filenames. No downstream document is structurally disadvantaged by metadata-over-identifier. The alternative (encoding authority in the name) would create cascading rename obligations on every promotion, which directly undermines the stability principle in § 1. **Risk level: clean.** Lock this in. The design is correct for an institution where artifacts promote frequently and references must remain stable.

**Check 3 — Composition and resolution rule strength.** § 3 specifies fully qualified references (`{artifact-id} § {section} :: {primitive-id}`), scope-defaulting rules, cross-track references, and alias governance. The bounded-context builder test: can a cold-start model, given only the ratified grammar documents and a handoff, resolve a cross-reference it encounters in an artifact? Walking a test case: a builder reads a delta entry anchored to `[aider:repo-map-context]` and needs to find the scaffold primitive. § 3.2 says unqualified primitive identifiers resolve against the canonical scaffold for that surface. The builder looks up `aider`'s canonical scaffold (the highest-authority enduring scaffold whose scope includes Aider), finds `HARNESS-TAXONOMY.md`, and locates the primitive. This works. A second test: a builder encounters `KNOWLEDGE-ARCHITECTURE-ROOT.md § 3` in a cross-reference. § 3.1 says this is a fully qualified section reference. Resolution is direct. One gap: § 3.2's scope-defaulting rule says "the canonical scaffold is the highest-authority enduring descriptive scaffold whose scope includes that surface." How does the builder *find* which scaffold covers which surface? The resolution depends on scaffolds declaring their scope (which ARTIFACT-GRAMMAR.md § 2.2 requires) and on the readiness matrix or some registry providing a surface-to-scaffold mapping. This mapping doesn't exist yet. **Risk level: low.** The gap is real but downstream — READINESS-MATRIX.md or a future surface-scaffold registry will provide the lookup table. The resolution *rules* are correct; the resolution *infrastructure* is incomplete. Acceptable at this stage.

**Check 4 — Signal identifier formats as build target for DELTA-METABOLISM.md.** § 5.6 specifies `SRR:{delta-entry-id}→{artifact-id}:{change-category}` and `SD:{surface-slug}:{primitive-name}:{staleness-grade}`. The question: should these formats be frozen now, or treated as provisional syntax under a stable semantic contract? Arguments for freezing: the semantic contract (from ARTIFACT-GRAMMAR.md § 5) is ratified, and DELTA-METABOLISM.md needs a concrete format to build against. Arguments for provisional: signal identifiers are ephemeral (§ 2.6 says they are "consumed on processing"), no existing system produces or parses them yet, and the first operational use may reveal that the format needs adjustment. The Unicode arrow `→` is a real encoding concern for plain-text tooling and git diffs. **Recommendation: treat the semantic contract as frozen and the syntax as provisional-but-operational.** DELTA-METABOLISM.md should build against the current syntax with a noted revision gate: if operational implementation reveals that the format needs adjustment (e.g., `→` replaced with `->` for ASCII compatibility), the adjustment is a bounded naming revision, not an architectural change. The five change categories and three staleness grades are semantic commitments that should be frozen. **Risk level: low if the provisional-syntax note is preserved.** The handoff already carries this note.

**Check 5 — Compaction/compression resolution.** § 4.3 draws the boundary: compaction is a temporal-track process operating on ledger entries; compression is a structural-track artifact class operating on higher-resolution artifacts. Completeness test: does this distinction hold across all downstream usage? DELTA-METABOLISM.md will specify the compaction process — it operates on ledger-class artifacts, produces compaction snapshots, and lives within the temporal track. SCAFFOLD-GRAMMAR.md will reference compression only if scaffolds can be compressed — and they can, via the compression class (domain cards, registry entries derived from scaffolds). These are clearly different operations on different artifact types in different tracks. The terminology boundary holds. **Risk level: clean.** The resolution is complete and should not haunt later documents.

**Summary disposition:** No fatal structural flaw. The dual-form surface identifier is justified by structural context difference (Check 1 — clean). Authority as metadata is correct for referential stability (Check 2 — clean, lock in). Composition rules are strong enough for bounded-context resolution with one downstream gap in the surface-to-scaffold lookup (Check 3 — low risk, deferred to readiness matrix). Signal syntax should be treated as provisional-but-operational with the semantic contract frozen (Check 4 — low risk with preserved note). Compaction/compression split is clean (Check 5 — resolved). The document is ratifiable.

Now the construction brief:All three deliverables complete. The grammar-layer sequence now stands at four ratified documents with a fifth construction brief ready.

**On the review frame's signal-syntax question.** I recommend treating the signal syntax as provisional-but-operational — frozen enough for DELTA-METABOLISM.md to build against, but with a noted revision gate if operational implementation reveals encoding issues (particularly the Unicode `→`). The semantic contract (five structural-change categories, three staleness grades, scaffold revision requests and sensing directives as the two signal types) is frozen. The syntax is the part that might flex. This distinction is preserved in the handoff's Unresolved Contradictions section as "bounded naming revision, not architectural change."

**On the construction brief for SCAFFOLD-GRAMMAR.md.** The brief's hardest design constraint is the dual-purpose twelve-check schema: it must be testable (a validator applies the checks to a finished scaffold and gets pass/fail results) and generative (a producer reads the checks and understands what structural features to build). These purposes can tension against each other — checks specific enough to be testable can become too narrow to guide production, while checks generative enough to guide production can become too vague to validate against. The brief addresses this by requiring each check to specify: what property is tested, how the test is applied, what pass looks like, what fail looks like, and what remediation is required. That five-part structure forces concreteness without forcing rigidity.

The brief also requires the drafter to validate every check against harness-taxonomy.md as specimen. This is the grounding discipline: if a check fails when applied to the corpus's most mature scaffold, either the check is wrong or the specimen has a real gap that should be documented. Either way, the validation produces signal.

**On the dossier-to-scaffold bridge (§ 5 of the brief).** This section is the most operationally consequential part of SCAFFOLD-GRAMMAR.md for near-term institutional work. The fourteen existing surface exegeses are dossier-class artifacts sitting at roughly Level 1 authority. The grammar layer's next major production campaign after completing the nine grammar documents will be converting those dossiers into enduring scaffolds. The bridge section tells scaffold producers exactly what structural operations are needed for that conversion. Without it, the conversion is an improvisation; with it, it's a repeatable procedure that the method engine can later abstract.
