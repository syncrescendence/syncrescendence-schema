## Part 1: READINESS-MATRIX.md Ratification Review Frame

Six checks, tight.

**Check 1 — Four-axis sufficiency and non-overlap.** Axis 1 (tier/modality-class maturity) catalogs structural completeness of the institutional decomposition. Axis 2 (scaffold coverage) catalogs descriptive substrate. Axis 3 (method coverage) catalogs generative capability. Axis 4 (temporal-domain coverage) catalogs current-state awareness. Non-overlap: no entity appears on two axes. Cross-axis references exist (Axis 2 entries reference Axis 1 for tier context; Axis 4 references Axis 1 for freshness derivation) but references are relational structure, not overlap. Sufficiency: is there a maturity dimension the four axes miss? Grammar-layer document completeness is captured as Axis 1 Tier 2 ("IN PROGRESS — 8 of 9 documents ratified"). Artifact authority-level distribution (how many artifacts at each level) is not an axis — but it's a derivable query across Axis 2 and Axis 3 rather than a missing dimension. The four axes are sufficient. **Risk level: clean.**

**Check 2 — Entry schema maintainability.** Each axis has a declared schema with mandatory fields, types, and descriptions. The maintainability test: can a future updater add a new entry without inventing new fields or interpreting narrative guidance? Axis 1: five fields (tier, maturity, structural_basis, next_milestone, blockers) — straightforward, all entries in § 4.1 conform. Axis 2: nine fields including conditional ones — the most complex schema. The conditional fields (species, authority, check_pass_count, known_failures, remediation_status) activate when a scaffold exists and are absent for GAP entries. This conditional structure could cause confusion if not enforced. But the schema table makes the conditions explicit ("required if scaffold exists"), and the initial population demonstrates both patterns (scaffold-present for coding harnesses, GAP for everything else). Axis 3: six fields — straightforward. Axis 4: eight fields including conditionals — similar to Axis 2. One note: the `freshness_distribution` field in Axis 4 is typed as "text" (a prose summary of counts per grade) rather than as structured data. For a living registry, structured data would be more queryable (`FRESH:0,CURRENT:0,AGING:0,STALE:0`). This is a format refinement, not a structural flaw — the field captures the right information in a slightly loose format. **Risk level: clean, with one format refinement opportunity on Axis 4 freshness_distribution.**

**Check 3 — Surface-to-scaffold lookup as standing infrastructure.** Axis 2 catalogs 45+ surface entities, each with either a scaffold identifier or a GAP marker. The standing-infrastructure test: when a new surface is added to the Teleology Registry, does the maintenance rule ensure it gets an Axis 2 entry? § 5.1 specifies that the matrix is updated "when a surface is added to or removed from the Teleology Registry (Axis 2 entry added)." This trigger ensures new surfaces get Axis 2 rows. The lookup is complete for all currently known surfaces. The mechanism for keeping it complete over time is specified. **Risk level: clean.**

**Check 4 — Coverage vs. quality separation.** Axis 2's schema has `scaffold_id` (identifier or GAP) for coverage and `check_pass_count` / `known_failures` for quality. These are distinct fields in the same entry. The initial population demonstrates both patterns: coding-harness entries show scaffold present with 5/12 checks and listed failures; all other entries show GAP with the quality fields absent. A consumer querying "which surfaces lack scaffolds?" reads the `scaffold_id` field. A consumer querying "which scaffolds need remediation?" reads the `check_pass_count` and `known_failures` fields. These are different queries returning different result sets. **Risk level: clean.** The separation holds.

**Check 5 — Axis 4 emptiness representation.** § 4.4 shows a single-row table: "*(no operational domains)* — — — — Zero domains established under grammar layer." Below it, a clearly labeled "Recommended initial domains" table lists twelve candidates as reference, not mandate. The emptiness is represented as a structural fact (the grammar layer defines rules, not operational content) rather than as a deficiency. The recommended domains are tier-mapped and ρ-classified, ready for creation but not pre-created. This is honest: the temporal track has law but no content, and Phase 2 is when content arrives. **Risk level: clean.**

**Check 6 — Typed outputs for AUTHORITY-LATTICE.md.** The authority lattice needs to consume: per-tier maturity levels (to calibrate delegation breadth) and method authority levels (to determine which methods are trusted enough for delegated invocation). Axis 1 provides per-tier maturity as enum values. Axis 3 provides per-method authority as enum values. Both are typed, queryable fields — the lattice can reference them structurally ("at OPEN SLOT maturity, decision authority is X; at MATURE, decision authority is Y") without re-assessing maturity. The matrix does not assign authority, recommend delegation, or prescribe governance — it provides the state inputs that the lattice transforms into decision-rights assignments. **Risk level: clean.** The boundary is maintained and the typed outputs are sufficient.

**Summary disposition:** No fatal structural flaw. Four axes are sufficient and non-overlapping (Check 1). Entry schemas are concrete and maintainable with one minor format refinement opportunity (Check 2). Surface-to-scaffold lookup functions as standing infrastructure with maintenance triggers (Check 3). Coverage and quality are cleanly separated (Check 4). Axis 4 emptiness is correctly represented (Check 5). Typed outputs for the authority lattice are sufficient without authority drift (Check 6). The document is ratifiable.

**On the construction brief's central design choice.** The brief identifies five decision types (promotion, ratification, assessment, operational, architectural) and explicitly notes they may consolidate if the structural analysis shows they follow the same governance logic. My current expectation is that they consolidate to three: constitutional decisions (ratification + architectural creation — both irreversible, both operator-only), intermediate decisions (promotion to Levels 1–2, assessment, domain creation — reversible but consequential, delegable with operator review), and operational decisions (method invocation, entry creation, compaction, sensing — bounded, reversible, delegable under method constraints). The five-type taxonomy in the brief is the maximal decomposition; the drafter should consolidate where governance logic converges and keep distinctions where it genuinely diverges.

**On the delegation framework.** The brief specifies maturity-calibrated delegation: at OPEN SLOT or NONE maturity, operational decisions are centralized; at PARTIAL, bounded delegation under ratified methods; at MATURE or ESTABLISHED, broad delegation. This creates a natural expansion of institutional capacity — as the grammar layer hardens more substrate and the readiness matrix records higher maturity, more decisions become safely delegable. Phase 2 scaffold production starts with the operator closely governing early conversions (the dossier-to-scaffold method is Level 1 untested), and delegation broadens as the method proves itself through successful invocations.
