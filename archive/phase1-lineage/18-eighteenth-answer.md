## Pre-Draft Calibration

Five load-bearing design questions, kept tight.

**Question 1 — Can one schema govern three species without false uniformity?** Yes, but only if the schema is structured as a core set of checks that apply universally plus species-specific instantiation rules for checks where the species differ. The structural properties that all three species share: declared axes, identified primitives, epistemic labels, staleness markers, source attribution, scope/species declaration, no prescriptive content, cross-reference integrity, header compliance, downstream traceability. Where species diverge: what counts as "decomposition axes" (surface scaffolds use modality-class axes; layer scaffolds use the convergent/divergent/interaction structure; cross-cutting scaffolds use per-tier manifestation structure), what counts as "primitive inventory at appropriate granularity" (surface scaffolds decompose one entity; layer scaffolds synthesize across entities; cross-cutting scaffolds map a concern). The solution: the twelve checks test structural properties at a level of abstraction that applies to all three species, and each check includes a species-instantiation note where the test criteria differ by species. This avoids both false uniformity (pretending species don't differ) and schema fragmentation (producing three separate schemas).

**Question 2 — Where must species-specific requirements exist?** Three places. First, in the axis-declaration check: what counts as well-declared axes differs fundamentally by species. Second, in the structural-completeness check: a layer scaffold must have convergent, divergent, and synthesis zones; a surface scaffold needs none of these. Third, in the source-material check: a layer scaffold requires multiple surface scaffolds or equivalent evidence; a surface scaffold can be built from dossiers and primary sources alone. These three checks need explicit species branches. The remaining checks (identifier format, epistemic labeling, staleness markers, source attribution, no prescriptive content, cross-reference integrity, header compliance, downstream traceability) apply uniformly.

**Question 3 — How to keep the checks both validatable and generative?** Each check gets a five-part structure: property tested, test method, pass criteria, fail criteria, remediation. The pass/fail criteria make it validatable. The property-tested and remediation sections make it generative — a producer reads "this check tests whether every primitive carries an epistemic status label" and knows to assign labels during production; a producer reads the remediation and knows what to fix. The key discipline: checks must test *observable structural properties*, not *quality judgments*. "Are decomposition axes declared?" is testable. "Are decomposition axes well-chosen?" is a quality judgment that belongs in the production procedure's guidance, not in the validation schema.

**Question 4 — How to weight the dossier-to-scaffold bridge?** It should be a full section (§ 5 in the brief), not an appendix, but shorter than the validation schema or production procedure. Its purpose is operational: it answers the question "I have a report-slack.md; what must I do to produce a scaffold?" The answer is a structured delta list, not a procedure (the production procedure in § 4 covers the full procedure). The bridge section identifies the specific structural gaps that separate a dossier from a scaffold and points to the production-procedure steps that close each gap. Weight: roughly 15–20% of the document. It should be concrete enough that someone holding a surface exegesis can see exactly what's missing.

**Question 5 — How to keep the production procedure strong without rigidity?** The procedure specifies *what structural decisions must be made* and *what structural features must be present*, not *what order to write paragraphs in*. It is a sequence of structural operations (select axes, identify primitives, assign identifiers, label epistemics, initialize staleness, organize editorially, validate), not a writing guide. Each operation specifies its input, its output, and its completion criteria — but not its internal method. A scaffold producer using the procedure should know *what to produce at each step* without being told *how to think*. The method engine will later abstract the *how*; the scaffold grammar specifies the *what*.

**The twelve-check count held.** The structural requirements from ARTIFACT-GRAMMAR.md § 2.2 and the naming/addressing conventions mapped naturally to twelve testable properties without padding or artificial collapsing. The checks separate cleanly into three groups: six that test the scaffold's internal structural quality (Checks 1–6: header, axes, granularity, identifiers, epistemic labels, staleness), three that test its relationship to the broader architecture (Checks 7, 9, 11: source attribution, cross-reference integrity, downstream traceability), and three that test its formal compliance with the grammar layer's classification system (Checks 8, 10, 12: descriptive purity, species completeness, scope boundary). This grouping is not declared in the document — it would add structure without adding testability — but it's visible in the design.

**The species-specific instantiation design.** Rather than producing three separate schemas, the twelve checks are universal with species-specific notes on three checks (axis declaration, structural completeness, and implicitly the identifier slug convention for layer and cross-cutting scaffolds). This keeps the schema unified while acknowledging real species differences. The critical insight: what makes the three species *different* is not what structural properties they must have (they all need axes, primitives, labels, identifiers) but how those properties are *instantiated* (what counts as an axis, what the structural regions look like, what slug type is used). One schema, three instantiations.

**The specimen validation is honest.** Harness-taxonomy.md — the corpus's strongest scaffold — fails seven of twelve checks. This is correct and expected: it was produced before the grammar layer existed, so it lacks headers, formal identifiers, standardized epistemic labels, staleness markers, per-primitive source attribution, cross-reference integrity, and downstream traceability. These are all remediable gaps, not evidence that the schema is too strict. A scaffold produced *under* the grammar would pass all twelve at first publication because the production procedure (§ 4) builds toward passing every check. The specimen validation demonstrates both that the checks test real structural properties and that the grammar layer adds real structural value over pre-grammar production.

**On the production procedure's weight.** The seven operations are sequenced by dependency (scope → axes → primitives → identifiers → labels → staleness → assembly) but explicitly permit iteration. Each operation specifies its input, its transformation, and its completion criterion. The procedure does not tell the producer how to think about the domain, how to write prose, or what order to compose paragraphs. It tells them what structural decisions must be made and what structural features must be present at each stage. This is the balance between repeatability and flexibility: the structure is prescribed, the craft is not.
