## Pre-Draft Calibration

Five questions, tight.

**Question 1 — One grading system or two?** Two, explicitly declared as distinct. The entry-level freshness grades (FRESH/CURRENT/AGING/STALE from the précis) answer: "how old is this observation?" They are a property of every delta entry, computed from its observation date. The sensing-directive staleness grades (watch/stale/critical from NAMING-AND-ADDRESSING.md § 5.6) answer: "how urgently does this scaffold primitive need re-observation?" They are a property of the signal generated when a primitive's coverage gap exceeds its threshold. These serve different consumers: freshness grades are read by anyone loading the ledger (humans scanning context, agents filtering by recency); staleness grades are read by the sensing pipeline that processes directives. Collapsing them would force one grading vocabulary to serve both purposes, which would either make entry freshness too coarse (three grades instead of four) or make directive priority too fine (four grades where three suffice). The resolution: declare both systems, specify their distinct purposes, and define the mapping point — when a primitive's freshest entry crosses from CURRENT to AGING (entry-level), the structural track evaluates whether to generate a sensing directive at `watch` grade (directive-level). The two grading systems meet at the threshold computation, not at the vocabulary.

**Question 2 — Tier-derived threshold strength.** The threshold logic needs to produce a concrete freshness window for each domain without requiring per-domain ad hoc judgment. The mechanism: each domain maps to a tier (declared in the domain's header). Each tier carries a ρ-regime from LAYER-LAW.md (high-ρ, moderate-ρ, low-ρ). The ρ-regime maps to a freshness-window class: high-ρ tiers get short windows (days to weeks), moderate-ρ tiers get medium windows (weeks to months), low-ρ tiers get long windows (months to quarters). Within each class, the specific window is calibrated to the domain's declared decay rate (the précis's per-domain decay rates are evidence for initial calibration). This produces a two-step derivation: tier → window class → domain-specific window. The précis's 7/30/90-day values survive as the default calibration for high-ρ domains, not as universal law. The document should specify the derivation mechanism and provide a reference calibration table, making it clear that the table is initial calibration subject to operational adjustment, not constitutional law.

**Question 3 — Domain architecture scope.** The domain architecture section must specify *what makes a domain well-formed and how domains are governed* (structural law), not *which specific domains the institution should track* (operational configuration). The précis's twelve-domain list is evidence and initial recommendation, not canon. The section specifies: definition criteria (changes, matters, changes independently), lifecycle rules (creation, splitting, retirement), tier mapping (how a domain inherits its freshness threshold from a tier), and the structural requirements for a domain ledger file. It does not assign specific agents to domains, schedule specific sensing cadences, or prescribe which domains to create first — those are operational decisions that belong in teleological manuals or operational configuration.

**Question 4 — Contradiction preservation clarity.** The rule must be stated as an absolute structural constraint, not a best practice. Formulation: "A delta entry that contradicts a prior entry is appended as a new entry with a cross-reference to the contradicted entry. The temporal track never resolves contradictions — it preserves them as signal. Resolution is a structural-track operation: when contradicting observations meet the structural-change criteria, they generate scaffold revision requests that enter the structural track's editorial queue. The temporal track's job is to observe and record, not to judge or harmonize." The key phrase is "never resolves" — this prevents well-intentioned smoothing. The cross-reference to the contradicted entry makes the contradiction visible rather than requiring a reader to discover it by scanning the full ledger.

**Question 5 — Compaction strength.** The compaction protocol must prevent two failure modes simultaneously: context bloat (too many entries, exceeding usable context) and provenance collapse (compaction that destroys the evidential trail). The mechanism: compaction summarizes entries beyond a freshness threshold into a compaction snapshot, but the original entries are archived in a retrievable location (separate section, separate file, or git history). The snapshot carries enough structural information (entry count, date range, key observations, cross-references to significant entries) that a reader can assess what was compacted and retrieve the originals if needed. The constraint that makes this work: compaction is lossy for detail but lossless for provenance — you lose the full text of old entries in the working ledger, but you never lose the ability to find them.

Now the draft.Three things worth flagging for your review.

**The dual freshness-grading resolution.** § 3.2 declares two systems explicitly rather than collapsing them. Entry-level freshness (FRESH/CURRENT/AGING/STALE) is computed at read time from observation date and domain freshness window — it's a property of every entry, consumed by anyone loading the ledger. Sensing-directive staleness (watch/stale/critical) is computed from the gap between a primitive's last-observed timestamp and its tier's threshold — it's a property of scaffold primitives, consumed only by the sensing pipeline. The two systems meet at the threshold boundary: when a primitive's freshest anchored entry crosses from CURRENT to AGING, the structural track evaluates whether to emit a `watch`-grade directive. This keeps entry-level freshness as a four-grade system (matching the précis's proven operational vocabulary) and directive staleness as a three-grade priority system (matching the signal format already ratified in NAMING-AND-ADDRESSING.md § 5.6). If you find two systems excessive, they could be collapsed, but the cost would be forcing either the ledger consumer or the sensing pipeline to work with a vocabulary calibrated to the other's needs.

**The compaction-entry identifier format.** § 4.2 introduces `[DOMAIN-SLUG-C{NNN}]` to distinguish compaction snapshots from regular observation entries within the same domain sequence. This is a new identifier variant not explicitly specified in NAMING-AND-ADDRESSING.md — it extends the delta entry identifier format with a `C` prefix marker for compaction entries. The handoff flags this as a potential registration need in NAMING-AND-ADDRESSING.md. If you want naming-law purity, a one-line amendment to NAMING-AND-ADDRESSING.md § 5.4 would register it. If you prefer to treat it as a natural extension of the existing format (same syntax rules, same uniqueness scope, one prefix character distinguishing entry type), it can remain as the delta metabolism's internal convention without a naming amendment.

**The failure-mode section.** § 6 canonizes eight of the précis's nine failure modes. The précis's "snapshot mentality" failure (entries replaced instead of appended) is structurally prevented by the ledger class grammar's append-only discipline and does not survive as an active risk — it is noted as structurally mitigated. The remaining eight survive with varying levels of residual risk. The honest finding: the grammar layer structurally prevents some failures (snapshot mentality, contradiction smoothing via append-only discipline) and structurally detects others (stale sensing via directive accumulation), but several failure modes are behavioral and cannot be prevented by grammar alone (feedback staying in conversation, overconfident labeling, means-ends inversion). The grammar provides the slots; producers must fill them.
