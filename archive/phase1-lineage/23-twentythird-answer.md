## Part 1: METHOD-ENGINE.md Ratification Review Frame

Five checks, tight.

**Check 1 — Abstraction level stability.** The test: does any method in § 3 contain content that duplicates its upstream procedure specification? Walking each method: scaffold production (§ 3.1) — procedural steps section is seven lines, each naming an upstream step by section number ("scope declaration (§ 4.1) → axis selection (§ 4.2) → ..."). No step content is reproduced. Dossier-to-scaffold conversion (§ 3.2) — procedural steps name the six structural deltas from SCAFFOLD-GRAMMAR.md § 5, each by function, then references the scaffold production method's step 7 for assembly. No delta content is reproduced. Sensing (§ 3.3) — procedural steps are derived from DELTA-METABOLISM.md § 5.3; the steps are stated as structural operations ("identify observation sources → observe → record → evaluate → generate SRR if criteria met"). This is the closest to duplication risk because the delta metabolism's § 5.3 describes the same operations in similar language. However, § 5.3 specifies what a sensing *task* must produce; § 3.3 wraps that in an invocation protocol with input/output contracts. The operations overlap in description because they describe the same structural transformation, but the method adds the typed contract layer. Compaction (§ 3.4) — references DELTA-METABOLISM.md § 4.2–4.4 for the operation sequence. Cross-entity synthesis (§ 3.5) — references SCAFFOLD-GRAMMAR.md § 2.2 for the three-zone structure. **Risk level: clean, with one calibration note.** The sensing method (§ 3.3) carries the most overlap with its upstream. If the operator finds the overlap excessive, the procedural steps could be compressed to a single line referencing DELTA-METABOLISM.md § 5.3 rather than enumerating the steps. The current form is defensible — it makes the method self-contained enough to understand without reading the upstream — but it's the one method closest to the duplication boundary.

**Check 2 — Catalog sufficiency.** Five methods plus two open slots. Completeness test: does the catalog cover every production operation the grammar layer has specified? Scaffold production — covers SCAFFOLD-GRAMMAR.md § 4 ✓. Dossier-to-scaffold conversion — covers SCAFFOLD-GRAMMAR.md § 5 ✓. Sensing — covers DELTA-METABOLISM.md § 5 ✓. Compaction — covers DELTA-METABOLISM.md § 4 ✓. Cross-entity synthesis — covers the layer-scaffold production path from SCAFFOLD-GRAMMAR.md § 2.2 ✓. Open slots: specification production and compression production — correctly declared as awaiting upstream procedures. Missing operations check: is there a grammar-layer-specified procedure that lacks a method? Scaffold revision (processing accumulated scaffold revision requests into actual editorial changes) is described in ARTIFACT-GRAMMAR.md § 5.2 and SCAFFOLD-GRAMMAR.md § 3 (the twelve-check schema governs the result), but no method wraps it. This is a genuine gap — scaffold revision is a repeatable operation the institution will perform, and it has enough upstream specification to support a method. However, it could also be treated as a variant invocation of the scaffold production method (re-invoking production against an existing scaffold with new evidence). **Risk level: low.** The gap is real but addressable in two ways: either add a sixth method (scaffold revision) or note it as an invocation variant of the scaffold production method. The operator should decide which treatment is correct. The catalog is not inflated — every included method has operational justification and upstream backing.

**Check 3 — Method/invocation distinction boundary.** § 2.6 defines the distinction in one paragraph, uses "method" and "invocation" consistently throughout, and explicitly states: "This document defines no invocation-tracking apparatus — that belongs to the readiness matrix or to future operational specifications." Does the distinction create downstream registration requirements? The readiness matrix will need to track method coverage (which methods exist, at what authority level) and may optionally track invocation history (which methods have been invoked, how many times, with what results). The method/invocation distinction enables this tracking but does not require it — READINESS-MATRIX.md can catalog methods without cataloging invocations. No naming-law registration is needed for the distinction itself (it's a conceptual definition, not an identifier type). **Risk level: clean.** The distinction is correctly bounded. It creates no downstream registration requirement. The readiness matrix inherits the option to track invocations but is not obligated to.

**Check 4 — Lifecycle concreteness.** § 4 specifies four lifecycle stages: creation (§ 4.1), validation (§ 4.2), revision (§ 4.3), retirement (§ 4.4). Concreteness test: can a method creator, validator, and operator each determine exactly what to do from the specification? Creation: "draft per § 2, enter catalog at Level 0, record creation" — concrete. Validation: "invoke on at least one real task, verify output against validation criteria" for Level 0→1; "invoke successfully across multiple tasks with diverse inputs" for Level 1→2 — concrete, with the diversity criterion preventing easy gaming. Revision: "triggered by upstream changes, operational experience, or structural-flaw detection; revised method earns authority from Level 0" — concrete on triggers, appropriately strict on authority reset. Retirement: "five-condition archival test from ARTIFACT-GRAMMAR.md § 4.3" — concrete by reference. One note: § 4.3 states the operator "may accelerate re-validation based on the prior version's operational record" for minor revisions. This is the one soft spot — "minor" is undefined. What distinguishes a minor revision (parameterization adjustment) from a major one (procedural restructuring)? The document does not define the boundary. **Risk level: low.** The soft spot is real but tolerable — the operator's judgment on minor vs. major is appropriate here because the cost of getting it wrong is bounded (worst case: a method re-enters at Level 0 when it could have been fast-tracked, or vice versa).

**Check 5 — Teleological-manual boundary.** § 5 states the structural constraint: "no method in this catalog contains a surface name, agent name, tool name, or scheduling directive." Scanning the five methods: scaffold production — no surface/agent/tool/schedule content ✓. Dossier-to-scaffold conversion — mentions "the institution's existing fourteen surface exegeses" as the typical input but does not name specific surfaces ✓. Sensing — mentions "primary documentation, vendor channels, operational observation, or research tools" as source types but does not name specific tools ✓. Compaction — no surface/agent/tool content ✓. Cross-entity synthesis — no surface/agent/tool content ✓. Survivability test: if the institution replaces every current tool, agent, and surface, do these methods still work? Yes — they specify structural operations against typed inputs, not operations against named tools. **Risk level: clean.** The boundary holds.

**Summary disposition:** No fatal structural flaw. Abstraction level is stable with one calibration note on the sensing method (Check 1 — clean). Catalog is sufficient with one addressable gap in scaffold revision (Check 2 — low risk). Method/invocation distinction creates no downstream requirements (Check 3 — clean). Lifecycle is concrete with one tolerable soft spot on minor-vs-major revision (Check 4 — low risk). Teleological-manual boundary holds under structural constraint (Check 5 — clean). The document is ratifiable.

**On the review frame's notable finding.** Check 2 identified a genuine gap: there is no scaffold-revision method in the catalog. Scaffold revision — processing accumulated scaffold revision requests into actual editorial changes against an existing scaffold — is a repeatable operation with enough upstream specification to support a method (the twelve-check schema governs the result, the structural-change criteria define the triggers, the production procedure's steps apply with modifications for working against an existing scaffold rather than from scratch). I recommended treating this as an invocation variant of the scaffold production method (re-invoke with existing scaffold as additional input) rather than a sixth catalog entry. The rationale: scaffold revision uses the same structural operations as scaffold production (re-verify axes, re-identify primitives, re-label, re-validate) with the additional constraint of an existing scaffold as both input and revision target. If the operator later finds that revision has enough distinctive structural requirements to warrant its own method, it can be added to the catalog through the method-lifecycle creation process without amending the method engine.

**On the method/invocation distinction.** Confirmed: purely definitional, no downstream registration requirement. The readiness matrix inherits the *option* to track invocations but is not obligated to. The brief for READINESS-MATRIX.md does not include invocation tracking in the mandatory entry schema — method coverage (Axis 3) catalogs methods and their authority levels, not individual invocations. If the operator later wants invocation history tracked, that's a readiness-matrix schema extension, not a grammar-layer requirement.

**On the construction brief for READINESS-MATRIX.md.** The brief's most important structural decision is the four-axis design. Each axis assesses a genuinely different dimension of institutional maturity: tier/modality-class maturity (structural completeness of the tier model), scaffold coverage (descriptive substrate coverage), method coverage (generative capability), and temporal-domain coverage (current-state awareness). These four axes cannot be collapsed without hiding real heterogeneity — the institution is simultaneously mature on Axis 1 for Tier 1 (ESTABLISHED), partial on Axis 2 (one scaffold exists with 5/12 checks passing), partial on Axis 3 (five methods, most at Level 0–1), and empty on Axis 4 (zero operational domains). A single "readiness score" would average these into meaninglessness.

The brief also requires the initial population (§ 4) to be honest about the Axis 4 emptiness: no delta domains have been operationally established under the grammar layer. This is correct — the grammar layer defines the rules, but the temporal track has no operational content yet. Phase 2 is when domains get created and populated. The readiness matrix captures this as a declared gap, not as a failure.

**On the final document.** AUTHORITY-LATTICE.md (Document 9) is the last grammar-layer document. It assigns decision rights per authority level and tier — who can promote, who can ratify, what delegation is permissible, and how decision authority scales with maturity. It depends on the artifact grammar's promotion ladder, the layer law's per-tier decision cadences, and the readiness matrix's maturity assessments (more mature tiers may support broader delegation). Once all nine are ratified, the transition criterion to the second wave is met: the grammar layer is complete, and the institution shifts from building the machine to running it.
