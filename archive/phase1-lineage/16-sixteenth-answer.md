**The dual-form surface identifier design.** This is the most consequential design choice in the document. The teleology registry already uses `slack_surface` format; the naming law and indexing spine need short slugs like `slack` for composite identifiers like `[slack:channel-object-model]`. I resolved this by declaring two forms — registry form (verbose, institution-wide catalog) and slug form (short, used in composition) — with the mapping declared once in the Teleology Registry entry and stable thereafter. The alternative was to force one format everywhere, which would either make the registry entries too terse for disambiguation or make primitive identifiers unreadably verbose. If you think this dual-form approach is overengineered and one form suffices, that's a straightforward simplification.

**Authority and lifecycle as metadata, not name-embedded.** This was a deliberate design choice to preserve identifier stability. If authority level were encoded in the name, every promotion would require renaming the artifact and updating all references. By keeping authority and lifecycle in the header metadata, the identifier survives the full lifecycle from Level 0 through pedigree without changing. The cost is that you can't tell an artifact's authority from its filename alone — you have to read the header. I believe this is the right tradeoff for an institution that promotes artifacts frequently, but it's worth explicit acceptance.

**Signal identifier formats are genuinely new.** The `SRR:` and `SD:` formats in § 5.6 have no precedent in the corpus. They are the syntactic realization of ARTIFACT-GRAMMAR.md § 5's semantic contract. DELTA-METABOLISM.md will build directly against these formats, so if they feel wrong — too verbose, wrong delimiter choice, wrong information encoding — this is the cheapest moment to revise them. The arrow `→` in scaffold revision requests is a Unicode character that may cause encoding headaches in some tooling; if that's a concern, a plain-ASCII alternative like `->` or `>>` works structurally.

**The compaction/compression resolution.** § 4.3 draws the terminological boundary cleanly: compaction is a process (temporal track, operates on ledger entries), compression is a class (structural track, operates on higher-resolution artifacts). This resolves the proximity flagged in the ARTIFACT-GRAMMAR review frame. The distinction should hold — the two concepts genuinely differ on every axis (track, input type, output type, lifecycle).
