## Pre-Draft Calibration

Five questions, tight.

**Question 1 — Abstraction level.** The right level is the invocation contract: what you need to start, what steps you perform (by reference to upstream documents, not by restating them), what you must produce, and how you verify the output. Each method in the initial set should read as: "To invoke this method, supply these typed inputs. The method executes the procedure specified in [upstream document § N], parameterized by [what varies]. The output must be [artifact class] passing [validation criteria]." The step-reference layer points to the upstream procedure's numbered operations without reproducing their content — a method consumer reads the method engine to know what to invoke and what to expect, then reads the upstream document to know how to execute each step. If the method engine contains any paragraph that could be deleted because the same content exists in the scaffold grammar or delta metabolism, that paragraph is procedure duplication and should be replaced with a reference.

**Question 2 — Initial method set sufficiency.** Five methods are specified in the brief: scaffold production, dossier-to-scaffold conversion, sensing, compaction, and cross-entity synthesis. Are these sufficient as a founding catalog? They cover the three primary production operations the grammar layer has specified (scaffold production, delta sensing, compaction) plus two derived operations (dossier conversion as a specialized scaffold-production variant, cross-entity synthesis as the layer-scaffold production path). Missing from this set: a method for producing specifications from scaffolds (the scaffold → specification extraction path from ARTIFACT-GRAMMAR.md § 4.2), and a method for producing compressions. Both are real operations the institution will perform, but neither has an upstream procedure specification yet — no document specifies how to produce a specification or a compression at the operational level. The correct move: include the five methods that have upstream procedure backing, note the specification-production and compression-production methods as declared open slots in the catalog (methods that will be created when their upstream procedures are specified), and resist the temptation to invent procedures for them here. The method engine catalogs what exists and governs how new methods are created; it does not fabricate methods for unspecified operations.

**Question 3 — Lifecycle concreteness.** The lifecycle must produce concrete institutional objects, not abstract governance theory. A method at Level 0 is a draft that has been written per the method architecture but never tested. A method at Level 1 has been invoked on at least one real task and its output validated. A method at Level 2 has been invoked successfully across multiple tasks and is relied upon as standard procedure. Each transition has a gate (artifact grammar § 4.1 promotion gates, applied to method-class artifacts). The lifecycle also needs a revision protocol: when an upstream procedure changes (scaffold grammar is amended), the method that references it must be evaluated for compatibility — does the method's parameterization still fit the revised procedure? This evaluation is triggered by the upstream revision, not by a calendar. Concrete enough means: a method creator knows exactly what to produce, a method validator knows exactly what to check, and a method consumer knows exactly what authority level to expect.

**Question 4 — Method/teleological-manual boundary.** The boundary must survive the moment when someone asks "but who runs this method?" The answer must always be: "The method specifies what; the teleological manual specifies who." The test: if the institution reassigns sensing from Perplexity to a different surface, does the sensing method change? No — only the teleological manual changes. If the institution changes how scaffold production works (amends scaffold grammar § 4), does the teleological manual change? No — only the method changes (to reference the revised procedure). Methods are tool-agnostic and agent-agnostic. This boundary is maintained by a strict rule: methods never contain surface names, agent names, tool names, or scheduling information. If any of these appear in a method, the method has crossed into teleological-manual territory.

**Question 5 — Method vs. method invocation.** Yes, this distinction should be named, but bounded. A method is the abstract reusable operation: the scaffold production method exists once in the catalog. A method invocation is a specific occurrence: "invoke scaffold production method for Slack, using report-slack.md as evidence, targeting surface-scaffold species" is an invocation. The distinction matters because the method lifecycle governs the abstract method (its authority level, its validation status), while operational logging governs invocations (which invocations happened, what outputs they produced, whether those outputs passed validation). The method engine should name this distinction in § 2 (method architecture) with one paragraph that defines the terms, then use "method" for the abstract operation and "invocation" for the operational occurrence throughout. It should not specify invocation logging, invocation tracking, or invocation registries — those are operational concerns for the readiness matrix or a future operational specification.

Now the draft.All deliverables complete. Seven documents drafted (six ratified, one pending). Two remain. Here's what matters.

**The method/invocation distinction.** Introduced in § 2.6, bounded to a single definitional paragraph. A method is the abstract reusable operation (governed by the method lifecycle, one per catalog entry). An invocation is a specific operational occurrence (governed by operational mechanisms outside this document). The distinction is named and defined but not operationalized — no invocation-tracking apparatus, no invocation registry, no logging specification. Those concerns belong to the readiness matrix (which will track method coverage and invocation history as part of its maturity assessment) or to future operational specifications. The distinction was worth naming because without it, the lifecycle governance in § 4 would be ambiguous about whether it governs the abstract method or each occurrence.

**The authority levels are honest.** Scaffold production is Level 2 because the harness taxonomy was produced by an identifiable precursor of this method and the grammar-layer build itself constitutes repeated successful invocation across diverse inputs. The other four initial methods are Level 1 (their upstream procedures are specified but the methods have not been invoked under the grammar layer) or Level 0 (cross-entity synthesis, which has never been executed at all under the grammar layer). The two open slots are explicitly declared as methods the institution needs but cannot yet specify. No method is overclaimed.

**The abstraction level held.** Each method in § 3 specifies its input contract, references its upstream procedure by section number without restating content, specifies its output contract and validation criteria, and declares its scope. The scaffold production method's procedural-steps section is seven lines long — one per upstream step, each a reference. If any of those references were expanded into the step's full content, it would be procedure duplication. The abstraction constraint survived drafting.

**No new dependencies appeared.** The remaining two documents still close Phase 1 cleanly. READINESS-MATRIX.md depends on everything before it (it catalogs maturity across the full grammar layer's dimensions). AUTHORITY-LATTICE.md depends on the artifact grammar's promotion ladder and the layer law's per-tier decision cadences. Neither requires anything that isn't already ratified or ratifiable. The transition criterion to the second wave — all nine grammar documents ratified, readiness matrix populated, method engine operational — remains achievable from the current position.

**On the readiness matrix.** It is the next document and structurally distinct from everything before it. It is a registry-class artifact, not canon or specification. Its job is to apply the maturity semantics defined in LAYER-LAW.md to produce a typed assessment of where the institution currently stands — per tier, per modality class, per method, per temporal-track domain. It absorbs the current-status assessments that LAYER-LAW.md deferred to it (the datestamped lines in each tier section). It also provides the surface-to-scaffold lookup that the NAMING-AND-ADDRESSING.md review identified as a gap. It is the document that turns the grammar layer's abstract machinery into a concrete picture of institutional readiness.
