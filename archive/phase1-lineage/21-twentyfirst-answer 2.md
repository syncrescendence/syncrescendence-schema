## Part 1: DELTA-METABOLISM.md Ratification Review Frame

Six checks, tight.

**Check 1 — Dual freshness-grading systems.** § 3.2 declares two systems: entry-level freshness (FRESH/CURRENT/AGING/STALE) and sensing-directive staleness (watch/stale/critical). The complexity test: does the institution actually need both, or could one system serve both purposes? Entry-level freshness is consumed by every ledger reader — humans scanning for recency, agents filtering context by freshness, compaction logic deciding what to archive. It needs four grades because the operational difference between FRESH (very recent, high trust) and CURRENT (recent enough, reliable) matters for context loading, and the difference between AGING (getting old, worth checking) and STALE (unreliable without re-verification) matters for compaction. Sensing-directive staleness is consumed only by the sensing pipeline. It needs three priority grades because the pipeline needs to triage: `watch` (schedule observation soon), `stale` (observe now), `critical` (urgent, blocking). These are different consumers with different decision granularity. Collapsing them would either give context-loaders too few grades (three is too coarse for context-loading triage) or give the sensing pipeline too many (four priority levels is unnecessary overhead for a processing queue). The mapping point between systems (AGING entry triggers `watch`-grade evaluation) is clean and unidirectional. **Risk level: clean.** Two systems is the correct design. The complexity is justified by distinct consumers with distinct granularity needs.

**Check 2 — Tier-derived threshold concreteness.** § 2.3 specifies three ρ-regime classes with default calibrations. The law-vs-guidance test: can a domain creator determine their domain's freshness windows from the specification alone, without ad hoc judgment? Walking the process: creator declares domain, maps it to a tier, looks up the tier's ρ-regime in LAYER-LAW.md, selects the corresponding freshness-window class from § 2.3, applies the default calibration. This produces concrete numbers. The default calibrations (high-ρ: 7/21/60 days; moderate-ρ: 14/45/120 days; low-ρ: 30/90/270 days) are specific enough to function as starting values. The document permits operational recalibration, recorded as an editorial act in the domain header — this is appropriate flexibility, not vagueness. One note: the default calibrations differ from the précis's 7/30/90 for high-ρ domains (the delta metabolism uses 7/21/60). This is a deliberate recalibration, not an error — the grammar layer shortens the CURRENT window to account for the higher refresh cadence that high-ρ domains require. **Risk level: clean.** The thresholds are concrete enough to function as law with operational adjustment rights.

**Check 3 — Domain architecture as law vs. configuration.** § 2 specifies definition criteria, lifecycle rules, tier mapping, and structural requirements for domain ledger files. Does it stay on the law side? Walking the section: § 2.1 (definition criteria) — structural law, specifying what makes a domain valid. § 2.2 (lifecycle) — structural law, specifying how domains are created, split, and retired. § 2.3 (tier mapping) — structural law with reference calibration tables. No section assigns specific agents, schedules specific sensing cadences, or prescribes which domains to create. The précis's twelve-domain recommended set is not reproduced as canon — the document specifies the rules for creating domains, not the domains themselves. **Risk level: clean.** The section is correctly scoped as temporal-track law.

**Check 4 — Contradiction-preservation strength.** § 3.4 states: "The temporal track never resolves contradictions — it preserves them as signal." And: "The prohibition on resolution is absolute within the temporal track. No entry is edited to harmonize with a later observation. No entry is deleted because a subsequent entry supersedes it. No entry's confidence level is retroactively downgraded because a contradicting entry arrived." The strength test: is there any scenario where a well-intentioned producer could reasonably interpret this rule as permitting smoothing? The word "absolute" plus the three specific prohibitions (no editing, no deleting, no retroactive downgrading) close the main smoothing vectors. The mandatory `Contradicts: [DOMAIN-SLUG-NNN]` cross-reference makes contradictions visible rather than discoverable by accident. One residual gap: what about a producer who creates a new entry that *summarizes* two contradicting entries into a reconciled position, effectively smoothing via synthesis rather than editing? This is technically not prohibited — it's a new entry, not an edit to an old one. But it would function as smoothing if the new entry's confidence is HIGH and the old entries are allowed to age out. § 3.4 handles this through the structural track: "Resolution is a structural-track operation — it happens through scaffold revision, not through delta editing." A synthesized reconciliation entry at HIGH confidence would fail the overconfident-entry check (§ 6) unless the reconciliation is genuinely supported by new evidence. **Risk level: low.** The prohibition is strong enough. The residual vector (smoothing via synthesis) is mitigated by the overconfidence failure mode and the structural-track resolution principle, though not explicitly closed. Acceptable at this stage.

**Check 5 — Compaction protocol completeness.** § 4 specifies triggers (volume and cadence), operation (three-tier retention with STALE entries summarized into snapshots), provenance preservation (originals archived, cross-references maintained), and constraints (no contradiction resolution, no identifier changes, no retroactive editing). The dual test: does it prevent context bloat? Does it preserve provenance? Bloat prevention: the volume trigger (80–120 entries) and cadence trigger (calibrated to domain decay rate) ensure compaction fires before ledgers become unwieldy. The three-tier retention (FRESH/CURRENT preserved, AGING conditional, STALE compacted) is calibrated to keep active intelligence accessible while archiving historical observations. Provenance preservation: original entries move to an archive section or companion file, retain their identifiers, and are cross-referenced from the compaction snapshot. A reader can trace any compacted claim back to its source entries. **Risk level: clean.** The protocol solves both problems. The archive mechanism is straightforward (section or companion file), and the constraint set prevents the three main compaction hazards (contradiction resolution, identifier mutation, retroactive editing).

**Check 6 — Compaction-entry identifier registration.** § 4.2 introduces `[DOMAIN-SLUG-C{NNN}]` as the compaction snapshot identifier format. NAMING-AND-ADDRESSING.md § 5.4 specifies `[{DOMAIN-SLUG}-{NNN}]` for delta entries but does not cover the `C`-prefix variant. Should this be formally registered? The `C`-prefix follows the same syntax rules, same uniqueness scope, and same stability rules as regular entries — it differs only in the type-distinguishing prefix. Registration would be a one-line amendment to NAMING-AND-ADDRESSING.md § 5.4 noting that compaction entries use the `C`-prefix variant. **Recommendation: defer.** The format is a natural extension that follows all existing rules. Registering it now would mean reopening a ratified document for a single-line addition. Preferable to register it in a batch amendment when READINESS-MATRIX.md or the naming document's next revision occasion arises, along with the modality-class and concern slugs from SCAFFOLD-GRAMMAR.md § 4.4. Both are tracked as deferred registration items in the handoff's Unresolved Contradictions. **Risk level: none.** The format is compatible; registration is housekeeping, not structural.

**Summary disposition:** No fatal structural flaw. The dual freshness-grading system is justified by distinct consumer granularity (Check 1 — clean). Tier-derived thresholds are concrete enough to function as law (Check 2 — clean). Domain architecture stays on the law side (Check 3 — clean). Contradiction preservation is strong enough with one residual vector mitigated by other mechanisms (Check 4 — low risk). Compaction protocol solves both bloat and provenance (Check 5 — clean). Compaction-entry identifier registration is deferrable housekeeping (Check 6 — defer). The document is ratifiable.

---All three deliverables complete. Six ratified, seventh brief ready. Here's what matters.

**On the compaction-identifier question.** The `C`-prefix format is deferred as batch housekeeping. The handoff now carries a dedicated "Naming-Law Deferred Amendments" section that groups all three identifier variants awaiting registration: compaction-entry identifiers, modality-class slugs, and concern slugs. All three follow existing format rules and are natural extensions. They'll be registered in a single naming-law amendment when the next revision occasion arises — most likely during or after READINESS-MATRIX.md, which will need to catalog entities using these identifier types.

**On the construction brief for METHOD-ENGINE.md.** This document sits at a structurally unusual position in the grammar layer: it is itself an instance of the class it governs. METHOD-ENGINE.md is a method-class artifact that defines what method-class artifacts must contain. This self-referential property is declared in the brief's header (artifact class: method) and creates a natural validation test — the finished document must satisfy its own method architecture specification from § 2.

The brief's hardest constraint is the abstraction level. The five initial methods (scaffold production, dossier-to-scaffold conversion, sensing, compaction, cross-entity synthesis) must each reference their upstream procedure specification without restating it, wrapping the procedure in invocation protocol, parameterization, and output validation. The risk is that "wrapping without restating" collapses into either pure reference (too thin — the method adds nothing over the upstream procedure) or full recapitulation (too thick — the method duplicates the upstream). The right level is: input contract (what the method needs), step references (pointing to scaffold grammar § 4 steps 1–7, not reproducing them), output contract (what artifact class the result must be, what validation it must pass), and parameterization (what varies per invocation — target surface, evidence set, species). The method adds the invocation layer; the upstream document carries the procedure layer.

**On the remaining sequence.** After METHOD-ENGINE.md, two documents remain: READINESS-MATRIX.md (Document 8) and AUTHORITY-LATTICE.md (Document 9). The readiness matrix is a registry-class artifact that catalogs per-tier, per-modality-class, and per-method-coverage maturity — it depends on nearly everything before it. The authority lattice is a canon-class artifact that assigns decision rights per authority level and tier — it depends on the promotion ladder and the tier model. Together they close the grammar layer: the readiness matrix tells the institution where it stands, and the authority lattice tells it who can decide what. After all nine are ratified, the transition criterion to the second wave is met, and scaffold production for open-slot domains begins.
