## Pre-Draft Calibration

Five questions, tight.

**Question 1 — Consolidation of decision types.** The brief identifies five: promotion, ratification, assessment, operational, architectural. These should consolidate to three governance families based on shared governance logic. Constitutional decisions (ratification + architectural creation) share: irreversibility, wide propagation scope, operator-only authority, no delegation under any maturity condition. Intermediate decisions (promotion to Levels 0→1 and 1→2, assessment, domain creation, scaffold production) share: consequentiality without irreversibility, delegable with operator review or post-hoc verification, governed by method constraints. Operational decisions (method invocation for routine tasks, delta entry creation, sensing-directive processing, compaction execution) share: bounded scope, full reversibility, delegable under method constraints at sufficient maturity. The mapping from five types to three families: ratification → constitutional; architectural → constitutional; promotion (Levels 0→2) → intermediate; assessment → intermediate; operational → operational. The document should name the three families, then show the five-type decomposition as the internal structure of those families so no decision type is silently absorbed.

**Question 2 — Decision authority vs. execution authority.** This distinction should be explicitly named. Decision authority is the right to determine *whether* something happens — whether a scaffold is promoted, whether a domain is created, whether a method is revised. Execution authority is the right to perform the work — to invoke the method, to write the entries, to produce the scaffold. These are separable: an agent may have execution authority (it can run the scaffold production method) without having decision authority (it cannot decide that a scaffold should be produced for Slack — the operator or a governance process decides that). Conversely, the operator has decision authority over constitutional matters but may delegate execution to an agent. The lattice should specify both dimensions: who decides, and who executes. This prevents the common failure where delegation of execution is confused with delegation of decision, and an agent that was authorized to run a method starts autonomously deciding *which* methods to run on *which* targets.

**Question 3 — Maturity-calibrated delegation as law.** The delegation framework must produce concrete, testable delegation rules rather than advisory language. The formulation: for each governance family, at each maturity level, specify the delegation state as one of three: CENTRALIZED (operator decides and may execute or delegate execution), DELEGABLE-WITH-REVIEW (agent may decide and execute, operator reviews output before institutional effect), DELEGABLE (agent may decide and execute within method constraints, output validated against method's output contract). These are enumerated states, not qualitative guidance. A cold-start builder reading the lattice can determine, for any decision at any maturity level, whether it is centralized, delegable with review, or fully delegable. The readiness matrix provides the maturity level; the lattice provides the mapping; the result is a concrete delegation state.

**Question 4 — Sovereignty preservation without over-centralization.** The risk of under-centralization: agents make constitutional decisions, and the institution loses sovereignty. The risk of over-centralization: the operator becomes a bottleneck, high-ρ operational tiers stall, and the institution cannot metabolize its own volatility. The mechanism that prevents both: constitutional decisions are always centralized (sovereignty preserved), operational decisions at MATURE tiers are always delegable (bottleneck prevented), and the intermediate category creates the governed transition zone where delegation expands as maturity increases. The key test: at the institution's current maturity (most tiers at PARTIAL or below, one modality class at MATURE), does the lattice centralize enough to be safe and delegate enough to be productive? The answer should be: constitutional decisions centralized (all grammar-layer amendments operator-only), scaffold production delegable with review (the method is Level 2, but dossier-to-scaffold conversion is Level 1 untested — review is appropriate), delta entry creation delegable (bounded, reversible, method-governed). This produces a workable Phase 2 operating posture without premature autonomy.

**Question 5 — Receipt constraint as governance mechanism.** The brief specifies a receipt constraint: every delegated decision must emit a verifiable receipt. This is the mechanism that makes delegation governable rather than merely permitted. Without receipts, delegation is trust-based and unverifiable. With receipts, the operator can verify delegated work after the fact without being in the decision loop. The receipt types are already defined by upstream documents: scaffolds validated against the twelve-check schema, delta entries validated against DELTA-METABOLISM.md § 3.1, method outputs validated against method output contracts. The lattice should specify receipt requirements per governance family rather than inventing a new receipt system — the upstream grammar documents already define what "verifiable output" means for each operation.

Now the draft.The final grammar-layer document is drafted. Nine documents, from constitutional root to authority lattice, spanning the full arc from "what kind of system is this" through "who can decide what." Here's what matters for your review.

**The consolidation held at three families.** Five decision types map cleanly to three governance families: Constitutional (ratification + architectural creation — irreversible, wide-propagation, always centralized), Intermediate (promotion, assessment, production commissioning — consequential but reversible, always at least REVIEW), Operational (method invocation, routine recording — bounded, reversible, maturity-scaled). The five-type decomposition is preserved in § 2.4's mapping table so no type is silently absorbed. The consolidation reflects genuine governance-logic convergence: ratification and architectural creation share every governance property; promotion and assessment share every governance property; method invocation and routine recording share every governance property.

**The decision/execution distinction is explicit.** § 1 names it, defines it, and states the failure mode it prevents (conflating delegation of execution with delegation of decision). The practical consequence: the Phase 2 operating posture in § 6 can say "the operator commissions scaffold production [decision authority], the agent executes the method [execution authority], the operator reviews the output [verification]." Without this distinction, "delegate scaffold production" is ambiguous between "the agent decides what to scaffold" and "the agent runs the method the operator commissioned."

**Intermediate decisions never reach DELEGATED.** This is the most consequential governance constraint in the document. The maturity-calibrated delegation table (§ 3.2) keeps intermediate decisions at REVIEW even at MATURE maturity. The reasoning: promotion, assessment, and production commissioning all involve judgment that consumes structural criteria from multiple upstream documents — they are not reducible to method output-contract validation. The operator's review load decreases at higher maturity (the structural substrate provides clearer criteria, making review faster), but the review gate itself persists. If you believe certain intermediate decisions should eventually reach DELEGATED at very high maturity, that's a one-cell amendment to the delegation table — the framework supports it, the current calibration is conservative.

**§ 6 is a demonstration, not a plan.** The Phase 2 operating posture applies the lattice's rules to the readiness matrix's current state and produces a concrete governance picture: what is centralized, what is delegable with review, what the first production targets are. It demonstrates that the lattice is operational — you can take the readiness matrix as input and the delegation table as law and produce a deterministic governance output for any institutional state. It does not sequence actions, assign timelines, or prioritize targets. Those are operational decisions that the lattice governs but does not make.
