Good. This is very strong. The descriptive quarry framing is correct. The permanent scaffold is the right concept. The research-packet grammar is right. The priority ordering is right. I want to push it one layer deeper now.

I do not just want the positive grammar of the permanent scaffold. I also want the negative grammar, the validation grammar, and the spawn grammar.

In other words: not just what an enduring descriptive scaffold should contain, but what it must actively refuse, how we know it is structurally complete enough to enter the permanent layer, and what downstream artifact classes each part of it is supposed to generate.

Right now I think you have defined the quarry well, but not yet the anti-fragility contract of the quarry.

So I want you to extend your model in six ways.

First, define the negative grammar of a permanent scaffold. What must never appear in one? I want this explicit. Not just "no rhetorical padding" in passing, but a real anti-pattern grammar at the artifact level: what kinds of prose, evidence abuse, structural ambiguity, narrative contamination, provenance theater, or hidden assumptions would disqualify a document from becoming enduring descriptive substrate.

Second, define the validation grammar. I want a way to tell whether a descriptive artifact has actually reached scaffold-grade. In other words, what are the structural checks? Does it have domain boundary, fixed axes, primitive floor, stable identifiers, inline epistemic labels, query handles, compression endcaps, open slots, and explicit downstream generativity? I want the checklist or schema, not just the intuition.

Third, I think "permanent scaffold" is actually too singular. I want you to test whether there are at least three species here:
surface scaffolds,
layer scaffolds,
and cross-cutting scaffolds.
My intuition is that a Slack exegesis and a trust-boundary topology are not the same species of enduring substrate, even if both are descriptive and permanent. I want you to formalize that distinction if it holds.

Fourth, define the spawn grammar. For each section or axis of a scaffold, what can it generate downstream? Registry row? Teleological manual? Adapter/manipulation contract? Canonical law? Ontology entity? Routing rule? If a section does not obviously generate anything downstream, I want to know whether it is actually needed or whether it is residue from an older, more essayistic research style.

Fifth, push the model-agnostic traversal idea further. The endcaps and identifiers are good, but I think the ideal document may need multiple compression depths built into the same artifact: full-resolution body, section endcap, domain card, registry-grade compression. Test whether that should be part of the grammar so different agents can traverse the same scaffold at different depths without generating redundant derivative artifacts too early.

Sixth, define the open-world extension law. We know the stack will grow. We are missing finance/accounting/ecommerce, HR-for-agents equivalents, backend/security layers, memory substrates, more orchestration and automation surfaces, and probably categories we have not even named yet. So I want you to specify how a new domain enters the system without destabilizing the grammar. What is the admission test? What scaffold species is required first? When does a new thing populate an existing open slot, and when does it justify a new layer?

The key thing I want to preserve is this: we are not trying to carry forward the baggage of the old prompting lineage. We are trying to abstract the generative machinery and replace the accidental cruft with something more incisive, more technical, more queryable, and more antifragile.

So the question now is no longer just "what makes an enduring descriptive scaffold?"
The question is: what makes the enduring descriptive scaffold layer itself self-correcting, extensible, and capable of generating the rest of the institution without reintroducing mush?

I want you to answer that directly.

And one more boundary to hold hard: the descriptive quarry is still not canon. It is permanent substrate. I want you to keep pressure on that distinction, because otherwise the strongest dossiers will start acquiring authority they have not yet earned.
